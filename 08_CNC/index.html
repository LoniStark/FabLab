<br> <h4>The Thinking Chair: CNC</h4> <br> I got it into my head I want to build a custom press fit chair. This is crazy given the compressed time and the other projects I am trying to iterate. But once it was in my head, I couldn't get it out. So we go on another exploration, against my rational brain... This idea didn't come from no where. The internet is a dangerous place and the inspiration came from looking at a couple of these sources. Yes, I'm putting the blame for the crazy of idea of building a chair on these influences. <ul> <li>https://www.pinterest.com/sarahk8ty/press-fit/</li> <li>https://www.behance.net/BGD</li> <li>https://www.ifitshipitshere.com/awesome-animal-furniture/</li> <li>http://fab.cba.mit.edu/classes/863.13/people/alice/week5.html</li> </ul> In particular, Boris Goldberg's design was beautiful and made me look more into the living hinge technique. I had seen some samples lying around the Harvard lab but up until now, I had not the courage to consider trying to do something like this. But give my brain a little victory in some areas and it starts to believe that all is possible again - my body and sleep schedule groan. Here's a great video on the topic: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JSlRusl7UPc. As I continued doing research, I found this great video about how to make Boris Goldberg's chair on a CNC machine: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f-WIeiTDWaQ. Here is a great video on the kerfing technique: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ngC5S5vWQ4g. With some of these ideas, I decided to make this chair personal to me and so I did some research on ergonomics of chairs. Using some of the information I found on these websites, I took my own measurements for the chair. <ul> <li>https://www.seatingmatters.com.au/how-to-take-accurate-measurements-for-a-new-seating-matters-chair/</li> <li>https://www.healthyworkstations.com/resources/ergo/TakingMeasurements.pdf</li> </ul> <img src="01_CNC_ChairMeasurements_Stark_July19.jpg" class="photo" alt="Measuring for a personal chair."> <figcaption>Measurements for my personal chair. Given my shorter than average stature, chairs usually feel a little too big for me. This is an opportunityt make a chair that fits me just right.</figcaption> <img src="02_CNC_ChairBackAngles_Stark_July19.jpg" class="photo" alt="Angles"> <figcaption>I also wanted this chair to be a place I could think, draw, and read in my art studio, so I do want some angle to it and not have it be a desk-type task chair. It was hard for me to measure what angle I would need tha back to be so instead I looked around the place I am staying at for the summer and picked the chair that I thought moved in that direction as a starting point. Looking at this chair also make me think of the angle of the seat and also whether I would have cushions. While I like the idea of cushions, given this will be in a semi-outdoor part of the art studio, the courtyard space, I was concerned cushions may attract outdoor wild life to nest in it. The preference would be to design a chair that would comfortable to sit in without any cushions. I also found this great reference: https://www.monsiegeamoi.com/blog/en/2020/01/06/the-ideal-angles-for-a-chair/.</figcaption> <img src="03_CNC_ChairSketches_Stark_July19.jpg" class="photo" alt="Measuring for a personal chair."> <figcaption>Based on the research, I tried to sketch out a chair design. First I created a stick figure of the chair with all the measurements I took. Then I overlayed it with a sketch that had the design elements I wanted. I decided to make the front legs assymetrical as I liked how it made the chair look like it was running off or posing, not standing still. Then extruded them into solids. I think I am going to go with a 3/4 inch plywood, double layer for the frame so that I can cut them in smaller pieces and then attach them by offsetting the two version. This will overcome the Shopbot limit of cutting up to 18'x24' wood. I plan to filet the chair frame in different areas, but I will do this later as I find if I do it too early, it gets more challenging to edit major aspects of the design in Fusion.</figcaption> I have some ideas about a single armrest that has a swing out table in case I want to rest a drawing pad or book on it. In particular, why they are symmetrical. As I continue to explore my "Thinking Chair" the idea of an asymmetrical design continues to grow in fascination. An aspect I love about asymmetry is that, in this case, you have to look at the entire chair to know what it looks like. One can't just see one leg and know what the other three are. Or see the profile of the chair and know it completely. I think this extends to people as well and this notion I continue to explore around self-complexity. I do think this is a chair that will invite me to think boldly and in interesting ways. There is still much to do to complete this chair. I feel the rush of time with each passing day. On one hand, it's quite a bit of self-imposed pressure. On the other hand, is there not the exhileration, like a firm press a car's acceleration, in rushing to express a vision? I had tried to draw this in on the digital version but it was not realistic enough to acertain if it would work with the design. I feel I've landed on the design and now it is about ensuring mistakes don't get into my CAD drawings and I can scale this up to it's actual size from the 0.25 scale prototypes.</figcaption> In terms of how I think I am going to accomplish building a real-life scale version of this within the boundaries of the time we have and the equipment we have in the lab: <ul> <li><strong>Two back legs</strong>: I think I will use the shaper tool cut the two back legs. In total the height of these parts are almost 100 cm which is 39" and beyond the 18"x24" limit of the Shopbot.</li> <li><strong>Front legs and braces</strong>: The shop bot will be able to handle this.</li> <li><strong>The seat</strong>: This is the hardest part as I wanted to use the kerfing technique to enable bending of the wood.
